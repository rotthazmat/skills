# Dimensional Analysis Guide

Full reference for Step 2. Each dimension includes a diagnostic question, what to look for, and an example.

---

## Causal dimension

**Question:** What explains or generates this phenomenon?

Look for: root causes, causal chains, feedback loops, enabling conditions, triggers vs. underlying causes.

Distinguish proximate causes (what directly caused it) from structural causes (what made it possible in the first place).

**Example — analyzing a protest movement:**
- Proximate: a specific government measure
- Structural: accumulated exclusion, absence of institutional channels for grievance

---

## Structural dimension

**Question:** How is the system organized? Who holds power, and over what?

Look for: hierarchies, distributions of resources or authority, formal vs. informal rules, who is included and excluded from the system.

**Example — analyzing an economic model:**
- Who controls capital? Who controls labor?
- What formal rules govern competition? What informal ones override them?

---

## Historical dimension

**Question:** How did it get here? What changed, what didn't, and why?

Look for: turning points, path dependencies (past decisions that constrain present options), failed reform attempts, long-run trends that persist across regime changes.

**Example — analyzing institutional corruption:**
- When did this pattern begin?
- What reform attempts were made and why did they fail?
- What colonial or pre-existing structures made it persistent?

---

## Empirical dimension

**Question:** What do the data actually say?

Look for: quantitative evidence, rates, trends, gaps between stated policy and measured outcome.

Use this dimension to ground abstract claims. If no data are available, signal it explicitly — don't substitute assertion for evidence.

**Example — analyzing inequality:**
- Gini coefficient trend over 20 years
- Social mobility rates vs. regional average
- Urban/rural income gap

---

## Interpretive dimension

**Question:** How is this phenomenon understood, framed, or disputed?

Look for: competing narratives, contested definitions, who benefits from each framing, what each framing makes visible or invisible.

**Example — analyzing a historical event:**
- Official narrative vs. marginalized narrative
- Academic consensus vs. popular memory
- How the dominant framing shapes what solutions seem legitimate

---

## Ideological dimension

**Question:** What function do ideas, values, or symbols serve here?

Look for: how ideology legitimizes or contests the existing order, which values are invoked and which are suppressed, how symbolic language shapes perception of the phenomenon.

Use when ideas are doing active causal or structural work — not just as background context.

**Example — analyzing a political speech:**
- Which values does the speaker invoke to make their action seem necessary?
- What alternative values are implicitly ruled out?
- Who benefits from those particular values being foregrounded?

---

## Choosing dimensions

Not all dimensions apply to every task. Use this filter before including one:

1. Does this dimension add a non-obvious explanation, or just restate what another dimension already covers?
2. Would removing this dimension leave a significant gap in the model?
3. Is there actual content to analyze in this dimension, or would it be empty?

Include only dimensions that pass all three. Skip the rest.
